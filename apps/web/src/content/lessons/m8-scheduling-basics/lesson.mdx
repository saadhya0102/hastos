# CPU Scheduling: FIFO, SJF, and Round Robin

The scheduler decides **which ready process runs next**. On a single core only one thread runs at a
time, so whenever the running job blocks, exits, or its time slice ends, the OS must choose a
successor from the **ready queue**. Different policies optimize different metrics, and the trade-offs
between them are a favorite interview and exam topic. We'll build up from the simplest policy to the
ideas your laptop's scheduler actually uses.

## Workload assumptions (and why we drop them)

Classic scheduling analysis starts with deliberately unrealistic assumptions, then removes them one
at a time. This is a great mental scaffold:

1. Each job runs for the same amount of time. *(dropped in SJF)*
2. All jobs arrive at the same time. *(dropped in STCF)*
3. Once started, each job runs to completion. *(dropped by preemption)*
4. All jobs only use the CPU (no I/O). *(dropped when we overlap I/O)*
5. The run-time of each job is known. *(dropped in MLFQ — the big one)*

The last assumption is the killer: a real scheduler **cannot see the future**, so it can't literally
run the shortest job first. Everything after SJF is about *approximating* the ideal without an
oracle.

## The metrics

We judge a policy by a few numbers. Let a job arrive at time `t_arrival`, first get the CPU at
`t_first`, and finish at `t_completion`.

- **Turnaround time** = `t_completion − t_arrival`. Submission to done — a **batch/throughput**
  metric.
- **Response time** = `t_first − t_arrival`. How long until the job *first* runs — an
  **interactivity** metric (you want a keystroke handled quickly).
- **Waiting time** = `turnaround − CPU_burst`. Total time sitting in the ready queue.
- **Throughput** = jobs completed per unit time. **CPU utilization** = fraction of time the CPU is
  doing useful work.

Turnaround and response time often **conflict**: policies that finish jobs fast (great turnaround)
tend to make some jobs wait a long time for their first turn (bad response), and vice versa.

## FIFO (First-In, First-Out)

Run jobs in arrival order, to completion, **non-preemptively**. Simple, and fair in the "first come,
first served" sense — but it suffers the **convoy effect**: a long job at the front makes short jobs
behind it wait, inflating average turnaround.

```
Jobs (all arrive ~0):  A = 100,  B = 10,  C = 10

Gantt:
A: ############################################  (0 .. 100)
B:                                             ## (100 .. 110)
C:                                               ## (110 .. 120)

completions:  A=100,  B=110,  C=120
avg turnaround = (100 + 110 + 120) / 3 = 110
```

If B and C had gone first, the average would plummet — motivating SJF.

## SJF / STCF (Shortest Job First / Shortest Time-to-Completion First)

**SJF** runs the shortest job first (non-preemptive). For jobs arriving **together**, SJF is
**provably optimal for average turnaround**. **STCF** (a.k.a. PSJF) is the preemptive version: when a
shorter job arrives, preempt the running one.

```
A = 100, B = 10, C = 10, all arrive ~0

SJF order B, C, A:
B: ##                     (0 .. 10)
C:   ##                   (10 .. 20)
A:     ############...### (20 .. 120)

completions: B=10, C=20, A=120  ->  avg = (10 + 20 + 120) / 3 = 50
```

That's 110 → 50 just by reordering. Now suppose the long job A arrives first and B, C arrive at
t=10. Non-preemptive SJF is stuck running A to completion (convoy again). **STCF** fixes it by
preempting A when the shorter B, C show up:

```
A arrives t=0 (len 100); B, C arrive t=10 (len 10 each)

STCF:
A: ##########            (0 .. 10)         <- preempted when B arrives
B:           ##          (10 .. 20)
C:             ##        (20 .. 30)
A:               ####... (30 .. 120)       <- resumes to completion

turnaround: B = 20-10 = 10, C = 30-10 = 20, A = 120-0 = 120  ->  avg = 50
```

### Why is SJF optimal? (exchange argument)

Suppose an optimal schedule ran a **longer** job `L` immediately before a **shorter** job `S` (both
ready). Swap them. Every other job's completion time is unchanged, `S` now finishes earlier, and `L`
finishes no later than `S` did before — so total completion time cannot increase. Repeat the swaps
until jobs are in shortest-first order: SJF. Hence SJF minimizes average turnaround for a batch that
arrives together.

The catch: SJF needs to **know job lengths** in advance (rarely true), and long jobs can **starve**
if short ones keep arriving.

## Round Robin (RR)

SJF/STCF are great for turnaround but terrible for **response time**: a job that arrives late waits
for everything shorter first. For interactivity we want RR, which gives each job a small **time
quantum** and cycles through the ready queue. No job waits long for its first turn, so response time
is excellent — at the cost of more context switches and worse average turnaround (every job finishes
later because they take turns).

```
A = B = C = 5, quantum = 1

Gantt (round-robin, 1 tick each):
A B C A B C A B C A B C A B C
completions: A=13, B=14, C=15   (roughly — they all finish near the end)
response times: A=0, B=1, C=2   (excellent!)
```

### The quantum trade-off

- **Too small** → responsive, but context-switch overhead dominates (each switch costs ~µs of saved
  registers + polluted caches/TLB).
- **Too large** → degrades toward FIFO: the last job in the queue waits a long time for its first
  turn (poor response).

With `N` jobs and quantum `q` (ignoring switch cost), the worst-case response time is about
`(N−1)·q`. Real systems pick a few milliseconds — big enough that switch overhead is a small
fraction, small enough that response stays snappy.

<Aside kind="pitfall">
Context switches aren't free even when the quantum is "spent productively." Beyond saving/restoring
registers, the new job runs with **cold caches and TLB**, so its first slice is slower. Shrinking the
quantum multiplies this hidden cost.
</Aside>

```
Overhead example: quantum = 10ms, switch cost = 1ms
  -> ~9% of CPU time is pure overhead (1 / (10+1)).
quantum = 100ms, switch cost = 1ms
  -> ~1% overhead, but response time for the Nth job grows ~10x.
```

## Overlapping I/O

Drop the "CPU only" assumption. When a job issues I/O it **blocks** — the CPU would idle if we just
waited. A good scheduler treats each CPU burst between I/O calls as its own mini-job and runs another
ready job during the I/O. This **overlap** keeps both the CPU and the disk/network busy and is why
interactive, I/O-bound jobs feel responsive: they run briefly, block, and are quickly rescheduled
when their I/O completes.

<Aside kind="insight">
This is the seed of MLFQ's key heuristic: a job that repeatedly gives up the CPU before its quantum
expires is probably interactive (I/O-bound) and should be kept at high priority; a job that always
burns its whole quantum is probably CPU-bound and can be demoted.
</Aside>

## Priority scheduling and starvation

Generalize SJF: give each job a **priority** and always run the highest-priority ready job
(preemptive or not). Priorities can encode importance, deadlines, or an estimate of shortness.

The danger is **starvation**: if high-priority jobs keep arriving, a low-priority job may *never*
run. The classic fix is **aging** — gradually raise the priority of jobs that have waited a long
time, guaranteeing they eventually run.

## No single winner

| Policy    | Optimizes        | Needs             | Weakness                         |
| --------- | ---------------- | ----------------- | -------------------------------- |
| FIFO      | simplicity       | nothing           | convoy effect (bad turnaround)   |
| SJF       | avg turnaround   | job lengths       | starvation; non-preemptive       |
| STCF      | avg turnaround   | job lengths       | starvation; needs preemption     |
| RR        | response time    | a quantum         | worse turnaround; switch overhead|
| Priority  | importance/SJF   | priorities        | starvation (fix with aging)      |

Real schedulers can't know job lengths and must balance turnaround *and* response — leading to
**MLFQ**, which *learns* which jobs are short/interactive by watching their behavior. That's next.
