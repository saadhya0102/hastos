# Multi-Level Feedback Queue (MLFQ)

SJF gives great turnaround but needs to know job lengths — impossible in practice. **MLFQ** is the
clever workaround used by real schedulers: it *learns* a job's behavior from its past and uses that
to approximate SJF, while still giving interactive jobs snappy response. It's the most important
practical scheduler to understand.

## The idea: priority from observed behavior

MLFQ maintains **multiple queues**, each at a different **priority level**. The scheduler always
runs jobs from the highest-priority non-empty queue (Round Robin within a level). The trick is how
jobs **move between levels** based on how they behave:

- A job that **uses its whole time slice** (CPU-bound) gets **demoted** to a lower priority.
- A job that **gives up the CPU early** (I/O-bound / interactive) **stays high** priority.

So interactive jobs (short CPU bursts) naturally float to the top and respond quickly, while
CPU-hogs sink to the bottom — approximating "short jobs first" without ever being told the lengths.

## The core rules

1. If priority(A) > priority(B), A runs.
2. If equal priority, A and B run in Round Robin.
3. A job entering the system starts at the **highest** priority (optimistically assume it's short).
4. Once a job uses up its time **allotment** at a level (across one or more slices), it's
   **demoted** one level.

## Gaming and starvation — and the fix

Two problems with the naive rules:

- **Gaming**: a sneaky job could issue a tiny I/O just before its slice ends to avoid demotion,
  staying high priority forever. Fix: track total **allotment** at a level (not per-slice), so it's
  demoted regardless of how it splits the time.
- **Starvation**: too many interactive jobs could starve CPU-bound jobs at the bottom. Fix: a
  **periodic priority boost** — every so often, move *all* jobs back to the top. This guarantees
  long jobs eventually run and adapts if a job's behavior changes.

## Why it matters

MLFQ-style schedulers power real operating systems because they deliver **good response time for
interactive work and reasonable turnaround for batch work, with no oracle knowledge** of job
lengths — purely from observed behavior. It's a beautiful example of learning a good policy from
runtime feedback.

## Recap

- MLFQ uses multiple priority queues; highest non-empty runs (RR within a level).
- Behavior sets priority: full-slice users are demoted; early-yielders stay high (approximating
  SJF).
- Track total allotment (anti-gaming) and periodically boost all jobs (anti-starvation).
