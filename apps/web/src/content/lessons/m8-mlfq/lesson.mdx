# Multi-Level Feedback Queue (MLFQ)

SJF gives great turnaround but needs to know job lengths — impossible in practice. **MLFQ** is the
clever workaround used by real schedulers: it *learns* a job's behavior from its past and uses that
to approximate SJF, while still giving interactive jobs snappy response. It's the most important
practical scheduler to understand, and variants have shipped in Solaris, older Windows NT, and
FreeBSD.

## The idea: priority from observed behavior

MLFQ maintains **multiple queues**, each at a different **priority level**. The scheduler always runs
jobs from the highest-priority non-empty queue (Round Robin within a level). The trick is how jobs
**move between levels** based on how they behave:

- A job that **uses its whole time slice** (CPU-bound) gets **demoted** to a lower priority.
- A job that **gives up the CPU early** (I/O-bound / interactive) **stays high** priority.

So interactive jobs (short CPU bursts) naturally float to the top and respond quickly, while CPU-hogs
sink to the bottom — approximating "short jobs first" without ever being told the lengths. MLFQ is
using the **past as a predictor of the future**: a job that has behaved interactively recently is
likely to keep doing so.

## The core rules

The full rule set (OSTEP formulation):

1. If priority(A) > priority(B), **A runs** (B doesn't).
2. If priority(A) = priority(B), A and B run in **Round Robin** at that level.
3. A job entering the system starts at the **highest** priority (optimistically assume it's short).
4. Once a job uses up its total time **allotment** at a level (across one or more slices, regardless
   of how many times it gave up the CPU), it is **demoted** one level.
5. After some period `S`, **boost** every job back to the top priority.

Rules 1–2 are the "how do we pick" rules; 3–4 are the "how do priorities change" rules; rule 5 is the
anti-starvation safety net.

## A worked trace

Three priority levels (Q2 highest, Q0 lowest), allotment = 10ms per level, RR quantum = 10ms at
each level for simplicity. One long CPU-bound job **L** (200ms) starts at t=0; a short interactive
job **I** (repeats: 2ms CPU then 8ms I/O) arrives at t=100.

```
t=0..10    L runs at Q2, uses full allotment -> demoted to Q1
t=10..20   L runs at Q1, uses full allotment -> demoted to Q0
t=20..100  L runs at Q0 (nothing else ready)
t=100      I arrives at Q2 (top). Higher priority than L -> preempts L.
t=100..102 I runs 2ms, then issues I/O (yields early) -> STAYS at Q2
t=102..110 L resumes at Q0 while I does I/O
t=110..112 I ready again at Q2, preempts L, runs 2ms, yields -> stays Q2
...         I keeps bouncing at the top (snappy response); L makes progress
            only in the gaps (reasonable turnaround for a background job)
```

This is exactly the behavior we want: the interactive job gets **low response time**, the batch job
still finishes, and **nobody had to tell the scheduler the job lengths**.

## Gaming and starvation — and the fixes

The naive rules (without allotment tracking or boosting) have two classic failures:

- **Gaming the scheduler**: a sneaky job could issue a **tiny I/O just before its slice ends** to
  reset the "gave up early" bit and avoid demotion, hogging high priority forever. **Fix (rule 4):**
  track *total allotment* consumed at a level, not per-slice behavior — once the budget is spent it's
  demoted no matter how it split the time.
- **Starvation**: if enough interactive jobs stay at the top, CPU-bound jobs at the bottom may
  **never run**. Also, a job that *becomes* interactive after a CPU-heavy phase is stuck low. **Fix
  (rule 5):** a **periodic priority boost** moves *all* jobs to the top every `S` ms — guaranteeing
  progress and letting behavior changes re-sort things.

## Tuning: the "voo-doo constants"

MLFQ has knobs, and setting them is more art than science (Ousterhout called such parameters
"voo-doo constants"):

- **Number of queues.**
- **Quantum per level** — often **short at high priority** (snappy interactive turns) and **long at
  low priority** (fewer switches for CPU-bound batch jobs).
- **Allotment per level.**
- **Boost period `S`** — too high starves; too low and CPU-bound jobs never stay demoted, losing
  the SJF approximation.

Some systems let admins configure these via tables (Solaris's dispatch tables), and some use
formulas (older FreeBSD decayed priority by recent CPU usage). The takeaway: the *mechanism* is
fixed; the *policy* is tuned.

<Aside kind="insight">
The high-level pattern — **start optimistic, demote based on observed cost, and periodically forgive
past behavior** — shows up far beyond CPU scheduling (e.g., adaptive rate limiting and cache
admission policies). MLFQ is a great example of learning a policy from cheap runtime signals.
</Aside>

## Why it matters

MLFQ-style schedulers power real operating systems because they deliver **good response time for
interactive work and reasonable turnaround for batch work, with no oracle knowledge** of job lengths
— purely from observed behavior. Modern Linux uses a different design (CFS, a proportional/fair
scheduler — next lesson), but MLFQ remains the canonical model for reasoning about the
turnaround-vs-response trade-off.

## Recap

- MLFQ uses multiple priority queues; the highest non-empty runs (RR within a level).
- Behavior sets priority: full-allotment users are demoted; early-yielders stay high (approximating
  SJF).
- Track total allotment (anti-gaming) and periodically boost all jobs (anti-starvation).
- Tuning the queues/quanta/boost period is workload-dependent.
